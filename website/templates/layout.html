An Analysis of the Opioid Crisis:

Opioid Use and Related Mortality in the United States: 2006-2012

Is there a correlation between legal opioid prescriptions and opioid overdoses in the US?

In 2018, the CDC lowered its life expectancy of Americans for the third time in as many years, the first lowering of US life expectancy since World War 2, citing drug overdoses and suicide among the reasons for the change. Opioid-related overdose deaths were the largest contributing factor to mortality.

According to recent investigative journalism, aggressive marketing for prescription pain killers and increases in opioid prescriptions for pain management, combined with an abuse of these prescriptions, created the opioid epidemic, a major public health crisis in the United States. In 2014, 61 percent of the 47,055 total drug overdose deaths involved opioids.

In August 2019, after winning a Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) lawsuit against the Drug Enforcement Agency, the Washington Post released a perviously secret database tracking all opioid prescriptions filled in the United States from 2006-2012. Because of the volume of data in the ARCOS dataset (Automation of Reports and Consolidated Orders System) and the potential life or death implications, the Post encouraged local journalists and big data practitioners to do further analysis and reporting. Our team sought to discover if it could find a correlation between legal opioid prescriptions and opioid overdose deaths in the US. To explore these factors we merged the ACROS dataset with CDC mortality data and performed a geographically-weighted regression.
